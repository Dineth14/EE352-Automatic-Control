Chapter 05

Root Locus

Tracing closed-loop poles as gain varies from 0 to ∞

Chapter Overview

The root locus is one of the most powerful graphical techniques in classical control. It shows how the closed-loop poles migrate through the s-plane as a single parameter (typically gain K) varies, revealing stability boundaries, performance trade-offs, and design opportunities at a glance.

Learning Objectives

  • Sketch root locus plots using Evans' construction rules
  • Determine breakaway/break-in points and angles of departure/arrival
  • Find the gain at any point on the locus using the magnitude criterion
  • Design controllers by adding poles/zeros to reshape the locus

◈ 5.1 — Root Locus Fundamentals

Characteristic Equation $$1 + KG(s)H(s) = 0 \implies G(s)H(s) = -\frac{1}{K}$$

The root locus is the set of all points $s$ in the complex plane satisfying this equation as $K$ varies from $0$ to $\infty$.

Angle and Magnitude Conditions

Conditions for a Point to Lie on the Root Locus
Angle Condition$\angle G(s)H(s) = (2q+1)\cdot 180°, \quad q = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots$
Magnitude Condition$K = \dfrac{1}{|G(s)H(s)|}$

◈ 5.2 — Evans' Construction Rules

The 8 Rules of Root Locus Construction
1. Number of branchesEqual to $n$ = order of characteristic equation (number of poles of $GH$)
2. Starting & ending pointsBranches start ($K=0$) at open-loop poles and end ($K→∞$) at open-loop zeros or infinity
3. Real-axis segmentsA point on the real axis is on the locus if the number of real poles + zeros to its right is odd
4. SymmetryThe locus is symmetric about the real axis (complex poles come in conjugate pairs)
5. Asymptotes$n - m$ branches go to infinity along asymptotes at angles $\theta_a = \frac{(2q+1)\cdot 180°}{n-m}$
6. Centroid$\sigma_a = \frac{\sum p_i - \sum z_j}{n - m}$ (intersection of asymptotes on real axis)
7. Breakaway pointsSolve $\frac{dK}{ds} = 0$ on the real axis (where branches depart or arrive)
8. jω-axis crossingsUse Routh criterion with $K$ as parameter to find the gain at which poles cross the $j\omega$ axis
Derivation: Asymptote Angles and Centroid ▸

For large $|s|$, $G(s)H(s) \approx \frac{K'}{s^{n-m}}$ where $K'$ is the ratio of leading coefficients.

The angle condition becomes $\angle(1/s^{n-m}) = (2q+1)\cdot 180°$, so $(n-m)\theta = (2q+1)\cdot 180°$:

$$\theta_a = \frac{(2q+1)\cdot 180°}{n-m}, \quad q = 0, 1, \ldots, n-m-1$$

The centroid is where asymptotes intersect the real axis. From the Laurent expansion of $G(s)H(s)$ about $s = \infty$:

$$\sigma_a = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(-p_i) - \sum_{j=1}^m(-z_j)}{n-m}$$
Angles of Departure and Arrival ▸

Angle of departure from a complex pole $p_i$: apply the angle condition at a point infinitesimally close to $p_i$:

$$\theta_{dep} = 180° - \sum_{k \neq i}\angle(p_i - p_k) + \sum_j \angle(p_i - z_j)$$

Angle of arrival at a complex zero $z_j$:

$$\theta_{arr} = 180° - \sum_k \angle(z_j - p_k) + \sum_{i \neq j}\angle(z_j - z_i)$$

◈ 5.3 — Controller Design via Root Locus

The root locus can be reshaped by adding poles and zeros to the controller:

  • Adding a zero (lead compensation): pulls the locus toward the left — improves transient response
  • Adding a pole (lag compensation): can improve steady-state accuracy without significantly affecting transient
  • Lead-lag compensator: adds both a pole and zero for combined benefits
Lead Compensator $$G_c(s) = K_c \frac{s + z_c}{s + p_c}, \quad |z_c| < |p_c|$$

The zero is closer to the origin than the pole, providing phase lead at crossover.
