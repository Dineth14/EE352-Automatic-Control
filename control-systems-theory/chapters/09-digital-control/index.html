Chapter 09

Digital Control

Z-transforms, sampling, and discrete-time systems

Chapter Overview

Modern controllers are implemented digitally. This chapter bridges continuous and discrete worlds — covering sampling, the Z-transform, zero-order hold, and discrete stability analysis where the unit circle replaces the jω-axis.

◈ 9.1 — Sampling & Zero-Order Hold

Ideal Sampler $$x^*(t) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x(kT)\,\delta(t - kT)$$

The sampler converts a continuous signal to an impulse train at period $T$.

Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) $$G_{\text{ZOH}}(s) = \frac{1 - e^{-sT}}{s}$$

Holds each sample constant until the next sample arrives.

Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem $$f_s > 2 f_{\max} \qquad\text{(or equivalently } \omega_s > 2\omega_{\max}\text{)}$$

If the sampling rate is too low, aliasing corrupts the signal irreversibly.

◈ 9.2 — The Z-Transform

Z-Transform Definition $$X(z) = \mathcal{Z}\{x[k]\} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x[k]\, z^{-k}$$
Common Z-Transform Pairs
$\delta[k]$$1$
$u[k]$$\dfrac{z}{z-1}$
$a^k u[k]$$\dfrac{z}{z-a}$
$k\,u[k]$$\dfrac{z}{(z-1)^2}$
$e^{-akT}u[k]$$\dfrac{z}{z-e^{-aT}}$
s-to-z Mapping $$z = e^{sT}$$

The left half s-plane maps to the interior of the unit circle $|z| < 1$.

◈ 9.3 — Discrete Transfer Functions & Stability

Pulse Transfer Function $$G(z) = \frac{Y(z)}{U(z)} = \frac{b_0 + b_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + b_m z^{-m}}{1 + a_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + a_n z^{-n}}$$
Discrete-Time Stability

A discrete system is BIBO stable if and only if all poles of $G(z)$ lie inside the unit circle:

$$|z_i| < 1 \quad \forall\, i$$
Bilinear (Tustin) Approximation ▸

The Tustin method maps $s \leftrightarrow z$ via:

$$s = \frac{2}{T}\,\frac{z-1}{z+1}, \qquad z = \frac{1 + sT/2}{1 - sT/2}$$

This maps the jω-axis to the unit circle exactly, preserving stability — but introduces frequency warping: $\omega_d = \frac{2}{T}\tan(\omega T/2)$.
