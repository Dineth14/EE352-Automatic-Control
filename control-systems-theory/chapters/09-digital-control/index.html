Digital Control Systems

Sampling, Z-Transforms, and Difference Equations

1. Sampling and Aliasing

When a continuous signal $f(t)$ is sampled by a microcontroller every $T_s$ seconds, we get a discrete sequence $f[k]$. According to the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, the sampling frequency $f_s = 1/T_s$ must be at least twice the highest frequency component of the signal to avoid aliasing.

$$ f_s > 2 f_{\text{max}} \quad \text{or} \quad \omega_s > 2 \omega_{\text{max}} $$ (9.1)

2. The Z-Transform

The $z$-transform is the discrete-time equivalent of the Laplace transform. It converts difference equations into algebraic equations in the complex variable $z$. The relationship mapping the $s$-plane to the $z$-plane is:

$$ z = e^{s T_s} $$ (9.2)

Key mapping properties:

  • The stable LHP of the $s$-plane maps to the inside of the unit circle in the $z$-plane.
  • The unstable RHP maps outside the unit circle.
  • The $j\omega$-axis maps onto the boundary of the unit circle itself.
